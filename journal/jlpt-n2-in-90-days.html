JLPT N2 in 90 Days: My Study Stack

I failed JLPT N3 two years ago. Not because I hadn't studied — I had. I failed because I studied the wrong things in the wrong order, then panicked in the listening section when the speed was higher than anything I'd practiced.

This time, I'm not leaving it to chance.

Why N2, and why now

N2 is the benchmark that actually opens doors in Japan for professional work. N3 says "I've studied Japanese." N2 says "I can function."

I'm targeting the July 2026 exam, which gives me roughly 90 days from April 1. That's tight but doable if the schedule holds.

The stack

1. Anki — vocabulary backbone

I use the JLPT N2 Core 6000 deck. 30 new cards per day, reviewing anything due on the same session.

The rule I follow: if I can't recall a card in three seconds, I mark it wrong. No partial credit. Hesitation means it's not really in memory yet.

30 new cards/day × 90 days = 2,700 new cards. With the pre-loaded deck's spaced repetition, I should have ~90% retention on the tested vocabulary by July.

2. Genki II + N2 grammar reference

I finished Genki I in Singapore. Genki II covers N4/N3-ish grammar. After finishing it, I switched to the Try! N2 Grammar textbook — it's organized by grammar point and has context-rich examples.

Approach: one new grammar point per day. Write three example sentences. Use them in the journaling below.

3. Japanese journaling

Every day, I write 3–5 sentences about my day in Japanese. Not sophisticated — just documenting what I ate, where I went, how I felt. This builds the habit of producing Japanese, not just consuming it.

I share these with a language exchange partner who's learning English. We correct each other once a week.

4. Listening — the N3 killer

This is where I failed before. N2 listening is fast and the questions are subtle — they test inference, not just comprehension.

My approach:

  • NHK Web Easy daily — simplified Japanese news, read and then re-read out loud
  • Shadowing: I use the Shadowing Japanese book, 10 minutes per day
  • JLPT listening practice tests: one full section per week from week 6 onward

5. Reading — newspapers and native content

From week 4, I started reading one NHK article per day at normal difficulty. The goal isn't perfect comprehension — it's getting comfortable with kanji-dense text at pace.

The schedule

WeekFocus
1–3Vocabulary + Genki II review + listening basics
4–6N2 grammar + reading introduction + shadowing
7–10Full practice tests + intensive listening + vocab review
11–12Mock exams under timed conditions

Honest mid-sprint check

I'm about halfway through the 90 days as I write this. The vocabulary is coming. The grammar is mostly there. Listening is still my weak point — I understand more than I did, but N2 speed still occasionally loses me.

The journaling has been the most unexpectedly useful piece. Producing output forces you to confront what you don't actually know versus what you think you know.

July will tell us.
