Eating Through Singapore's Michelin Guide

Singapore has something that no other Michelin market has: starred hawker stalls.

Hawker Chan — a soy sauce chicken rice stall in Chinatown — held a Michelin star for several years. S$3 for a plate. At the time, it was the cheapest Michelin meal in the world.

That single fact tells you everything you need to know about how Singapore thinks about food.

The project

I started this systematically, the way you'd approach any engineering problem: define the scope, build a tracker, work through it methodically.

The current Singapore Michelin guide has:

  • 3 three-star restaurants
  • Several two-star restaurants
  • Dozens of one-star restaurants
  • Over 100 Bib Gourmands (value picks)

I'm not trying to complete it before July — that would require a budget I don't have and a stomach I might. I'm working through the Bib Gourmand list, with starred restaurants as occasional treats.

What I've learned so far

Price is not a signal for quality here. The S$4 char kway teow at a hawker centre I've been going to for six months is better than some dishes I've had at places that charged ten times as much. The guide knows this, which is why they created the Bib Gourmand category.

The "best" version of each dish requires specialization. The best chicken rice is at a chicken rice stall. Not at a restaurant that does chicken rice and five other things. Singapore's hawker culture is built on this — one stall, one dish, thirty years of practice.

Context matters more than I thought. Eating laksa at a table in a Michelin-rated restaurant is fine. Eating it at a zinc-roofed hawker centre at 11am, sweating slightly, with a plastic stool that's slightly too short — that's the actual experience the food was designed for.

Highlights so far

Tian Tian Chicken Rice (Maxwell Food Centre) — the benchmark. Every other chicken rice gets measured against this. The rice itself is what matters most.

A Noodle Story — ramen-influenced Singapore noodles that somehow work. The egg is perfect every time.

Liao Fan Hong Kong Congee — this is where I started understanding what congee can be. It's not a sick-day food here; it's a precision dish.

The unexpected part

What I didn't expect was how much this project would teach me about the Chinese dialect groups of Singapore. Teochew food. Hokkien food. Cantonese food. Hainanese food. Each has a presence in the guide, each with different flavor profiles and cooking philosophies.

Taiwan's food is largely a synthesis of these traditions, brought by waves of immigration. Eating through Singapore's hawker culture is, in a strange way, archaeology on my own palate.

I'll keep going until July. Then I'll make a list of the ones I missed and come back for them.
