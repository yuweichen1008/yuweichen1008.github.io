# The Cross-Continent Engineer

My résumé has three countries on it. That's unusual enough that interviewers often ask about it — sometimes genuinely curious, sometimes fishing for whether there's a gap in the story.

There's no gap. The through-line is work. The learning, though, is about more than work.

## What each place taught me about software

### Taiwan — precision and craft

Taiwanese engineering culture — at least in the places I encountered it — has a particular reverence for doing things correctly. Not fast, not cheaply. Correctly.

This shows up in manufacturing (Taiwan produces the world's most precise semiconductors) and it shows up in software. Colleagues there cared deeply about whether the code was right, even when the deadline was close. Code review was thorough. Refactoring was respected, not avoided.

The downside: moving slowly when you should move fast. The upside: very few things shipping broken.

### Silicon Valley — speed and systems thinking

The Valley taught me to think at scale. Not just "does this work for 100 users" but "does this architecture hold at 10 million users, and if not, what do we rebuild and when?"

It also taught me about organizational design as a technical problem. Conway's Law isn't just a theorem — it's something you live inside when the team is fifty people and the codebase reflects six months of conflicting priorities.

Speed is prized above almost everything. This has real costs — technical debt, burned-out engineers, products shipped before they're ready. But it also means you learn faster, because you're getting feedback from real users faster.

### Singapore — context-switching as a skill

Working in Singapore means working with colleagues and customers across multiple time zones, multiple cultural communication norms, and multiple regulatory environments. Southeast Asia is not one market — it's eight or ten distinct markets with their own expectations.

This teaches you to hold multiple mental models simultaneously. What works for a team in Jakarta is different from what works for a team in Tokyo. You learn to read the room in different ways.

It also teaches you to write clearly. When you can't rely on tone of voice to convey intent, you get precise with words.

## What doesn't translate

Some things don't transfer between contexts:

- **The Valley's directness** can read as rude in many Asian business contexts
- **Taiwan's emphasis on hierarchy** can slow down startups that need flat decision-making
- **Singapore's proceduralism** can be frustrating when you need to move fast

The skill isn't learning which one is right. None of them is universally right. The skill is knowing which register to use when.

## The thing that doesn't show up on the résumé

After moving three times across three different work cultures, I developed something I'd call *calibrated skepticism about best practices*.

Every engineering culture has its best practices — the "right way" to write code, run teams, build products. And they're all somewhat right and somewhat culturally contingent. The Valley's best practices are tuned for growth-at-all-costs startups. Taiwan's are tuned for manufacturing precision. Singapore's are tuned for regulated, multi-market enterprise software.

None of them is the universal answer. The engineer who's only ever worked in one context usually doesn't know this.

Knowing this doesn't make you a better engineer automatically. But it makes you a more adaptable one. And adaptability, in this industry, compounds.
