# From Night Markets to NASDAQ

There's a specific smell to a Taiwanese night market at 11pm — scallion pancakes on a flat iron griddle, stinky tofu from three stalls down, and sugar cane juice sweating in a plastic cup. That was my childhood. That was where everything started.

I didn't grow up thinking about Silicon Valley. I grew up thinking about where to eat next.

## The realization

The pivot happened in university. I was studying computer science not because I had a grand vision, but because I liked solving puzzles — and code felt like the cleanest puzzle there was. No ambiguity. Run it and you know if it works.

My professors talked about the Valley the way my parents talked about Taipei — as a place where the real things happened. I started reading Paul Graham essays at 2am instead of sleeping. I told myself: *I'll apply for one internship. Just to see.*

## Getting there

The interview process for a US tech company from Taiwan is its own gauntlet. Resume in English, behavioral rounds over video call at 7am, whiteboard-style coding problems that feel nothing like the code you actually write day-to-day. I failed my first three attempts. Not close — actually failed.

The fourth time, I got a call back. Six weeks later, I was on a flight to San Francisco with two suitcases and a printout of an apartment I'd never seen in person.

## What no one tells you

The work was hard in ways I expected — fast-paced, high expectations, surrounded by people who'd been coding since they were twelve. But what surprised me was the *softness* of Silicon Valley. Everyone was polite. Feedback came in the form of "interesting idea." Nobody would just tell you if your code was bad.

In Taiwan, my professors would circle your mistakes in red and hand the paper back. Here, they'd say "let's explore this further." I spent my first six months not knowing where I stood.

## The first real ship

Eighteen months in, I pushed my first feature to production. It wasn't big — a small improvement to a user flow that maybe 0.5% of users would notice. But watching the analytics move, seeing real people interact with something I'd built — that was the hook. That was the moment I understood why people moved their whole lives here.

## What I took from it

I spent four years in the Valley. I shipped products. I saw companies scale from fifty people to five hundred. I watched startups die in a weekend.

What I kept:
- **Speed as a discipline**: if it takes more than a week, it's probably the wrong approach
- **Build for users, not for elegance**: the pretty code that nobody uses is just art
- **Disagree and commit**: say your piece, then row in the same direction

Taiwan gave me roots and taste. Silicon Valley gave me velocity.

The next chapter was somewhere I never expected to end up.
