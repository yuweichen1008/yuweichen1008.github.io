# Building SkyReal: Year One

The romanticized version of founding a startup: you have an idea, you build it, people love it, you raise money, you scale.

The real version: you have an idea, you build it, nobody cares, you rebuild it, a few people care, you can't figure out why the others don't, you run out of money, you find more money, you rebuild it again.

Both versions are real. They just happen in parallel.

## Why SkyReal

I don't want to get too deep into what SkyReal does in this post — that's a different essay. But the *why* matters.

I kept bumping into the same problem when working with distributed teams in the States and in Asia: the tools we had for remote collaboration were built for Western workflows and Western time zones. They assumed everyone was online at the same time. They assumed everyone used the same stack. They assumed "async" meant "I'll reply in four hours."

That's not how teams in Singapore, Taipei, and Jakarta actually work. The rhythms are different. The context-switching is different. The way trust is built is different.

SkyReal started as an attempt to solve that. I won't claim we've solved it yet. But we're closer than we were.

## What year one actually looks like

**Month 1–2**: You're still euphoric. You're going to change everything. You have a prototype in Figma and a pitch deck with a TAM that your co-founder described as "conservative."

**Month 3–4**: First users. First feedback. First realization that what you built and what they want are different things. You rebuild the core flow.

**Month 5–6**: The first real crisis. For us, it was a technical decision we'd made early that painted us into a corner at scale. Three weeks of nights rearchitecting the backend while still handling customer calls during the day.

**Month 7–9**: Something clicks. One cohort of users starts using the product the way you actually imagined it. The retention curve looks like a hockey stick instead of a cliff. You almost cry, then you go find the next problem.

**Month 10–12**: Fundraising. This is its own form of psychological endurance sport. You pitch the same story forty times. You get thirty-five nos that teach you more than most of the yeses you'll ever get. You learn to separate the signal from the noise.

## What nobody warns you about

**The loneliness of being the decision-maker**: every hard call lands on you. What to cut. Who to hire. When to pivot. When to hold. Even with a great co-founder, you both know there are decisions you have to make alone.

**Energy management, not time management**: the startup advice world obsesses over productivity. The real constraint isn't time — it's the cognitive and emotional energy to make good decisions under uncertainty, day after day.

**The gap between your vision and your current product**: this gap is always there, and it's always uncomfortable. Learning to work with it, rather than against it, took me most of year one.

## What I'd tell myself at day one

- Hire slower, fire faster. The wrong person in a small team does more damage than a missing person.
- Talk to users every week, not every month.
- The first ten customers tell you everything. They're also the hardest to get.
- Your first product is a research project. Stop trying to scale it until you know what you're scaling.

Year two is a different story. But year one is where the company actually gets built.
