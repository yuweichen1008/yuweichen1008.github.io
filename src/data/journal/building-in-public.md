# Building in Public: Why I Made My Life a Changelog

Software engineers have an unusually good vocabulary for the thing I'm trying to do here.

A changelog is a record of what changed, when, and why. It's not a highlight reel — it includes the patches, the deprecations, the breaking changes. Good changelogs tell you what the project looked like at each point in time, and they tell you honestly.

This site is my changelog.

## What I mean by building in public

I don't mean what it usually means in startup Twitter, which is: "I'm building something and I'll share the good numbers and occasionally a humble-brag about how hard it's been."

I mean: documenting the actual process of living intentionally. The 96-day sprint before flying home. The JLPT prep. The food log. The projects. The journal entries that don't have a neat conclusion.

Public, because accountability to an audience (even a hypothetical one) changes behavior. Private journals are where you lie to yourself. Public ones have at least some obligation to accuracy.

## Why an engineer does this

There's a specific mental model I picked up in the Valley: the idea that you should instrument everything you care about improving.

If you care about performance, you measure it. If you care about reliability, you track uptime. If you care about growth, you look at retention curves.

I care about living well across a set of dimensions — learning, movement, food, friendships, work. So I instrument them.

The daily stats (steps, sleep, weight via Apple Health) are the obvious version of this. The journal entries are the less obvious version — they're the qualitative data that the numbers can't capture.

## What I've learned from doing it

**You can't log what you don't notice.** The act of writing daily made me a more attentive observer. I started noticing things I'd been sleepwalking through — which neighborhoods I actually liked, which social situations drained me, which kinds of work I'd keep doing even if nobody paid me.

**Accountability works, but only for behavior, not outcomes.** I can commit publicly to writing every day. I can't commit to having something interesting to say. The discipline is about the inputs.

**The retrospective is the underrated part.** Six months from now, I'll be able to read back through this and see the arc. That's not available to you without the record. Memory is lossy and self-serving. The log doesn't lie.

## The experiment

I'm 96 days from JLPT N2 and returning to Taiwan. Everything I do in those 96 days is documented here.

At the end, I'll have a record of what this chapter actually looked like — not the version I'll tell at dinner parties, but the version that actually happened.

That's the product. Whether anyone reads it is secondary.
