# From Night Markets to a One-Way Flight

There’s a specific smell to a Taiwanese night market at 11pm — scallion pancakes on a flat iron griddle, stinky tofu from three stalls down, and sugar cane juice sweating in a plastic cup.

That was my childhood.

Not ambition. Not strategy. Just instinct: follow what feels right, one step at a time.

I didn’t grow up thinking about Silicon Valley.
I grew up thinking about where to eat next.

## The First Shift

University was the first time life stopped being automatic.

I chose computer science without a master plan. It wasn’t passion in the romantic sense — it was curiosity. Code felt honest. You either solved the problem, or you didn’t. No politics, no guessing.

But something changed quietly.

Late nights stopped being about exams and started being about possibilities. I found myself reading essays about startups and builders — people who weren’t just solving problems, but deciding *which problems mattered*.

For the first time, I realized:
There’s a bigger game being played somewhere else.

## The Decision That Didn’t Feel Big

I didn’t make a bold, cinematic decision.

There was no “I’m going to change my life” moment.

It was smaller than that.

*I’ll just apply once.*

That was it.

One internship. One attempt. Just to see if I could even get close.

## Failure, Repeated Clearly

The process wasn’t designed for someone like me.

Different language. Different expectations. Different rules I didn’t grow up learning.

* Resume rewritten in English, again and again
* Interviews at 7am, trying to sound confident while half-awake
* Coding questions that felt more like performance than engineering

I failed my first attempt.

Then the second.

Then the third.

Not “almost.” Not “close.” Just — no.

At some point, it stops being about skill.
It becomes a question of identity:

*Are you someone who keeps going, or someone who quietly adjusts their expectations?*

## The One That Went Through

The fourth time wasn’t dramatically different.

Same preparation. Same uncertainty. Same doubt in the background.

But this time, something clicked.

A callback. Then another round. Then silence that felt longer than it actually was.

And then:

An offer.

No celebration. No big reaction. Just a quiet moment of disbelief.

Because deep down, I knew —
This wasn’t the result of confidence.

It was the result of not stopping.

## The In-Between Space

Six weeks later, I was holding a one-way ticket.

Two suitcases.
An address I had never seen in person.
A version of myself I hadn’t met yet.

Everything familiar stayed behind — language, rhythm, the unspoken rules of how to exist comfortably.

What I carried forward wasn’t certainty.

It was something more fragile, but more important:

The willingness to step into a world where I didn’t belong — yet.

## What This Chapter Means

Looking back, this phase of my life wasn’t about success.

It was about **permission**.

Permission to try without guarantees.
Permission to fail without redefining myself downward.
Permission to choose a harder path without explaining it to everyone.

Taiwan gave me instinct.
Failure gave me clarity.

And somewhere between the two, I earned the right to take that flight.

The next chapter begins when the plane lands.
