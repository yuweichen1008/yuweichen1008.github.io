The Softness of Silicon Valley

💼 Work
🌐

The Softness of Silicon Valley

I thought Silicon Valley would be hard.

Sharp people. High standards. Constant pressure. A place where you either perform or you’re out.

I was ready for that version of the world.

What I wasn’t ready for… was how soft it felt.

Politeness as a System

No one tells you you’re wrong here.

They say things like:

  • “That’s an interesting approach.”
  • “Let’s explore that a bit more.”
  • “I wonder if there’s another angle.”

In Taiwan, feedback had edges. You knew when you did poorly.

Here, everything was rounded.

At first, it felt kind. Then it became confusing. Then, slowly, it became exhausting.

Because if no one tells you you're wrong— no one tells you you're right either.

The Missing Signal

I didn’t realize how much I depended on clear signals.

Grades. Rankings. Results. Even failure had structure.

But here, feedback was abstract. Performance was implied. You were expected to just know.

And I didn’t.

So I started filling in the gaps myself.

  • If no one praised it, maybe it wasn’t good
  • If feedback was vague, maybe I missed something
  • If I wasn’t sure, I assumed the worst

It wasn’t rational.

But it was familiar.

A Different Kind of Hunger

Looking back, I wasn’t just trying to do good work.

I was trying to feel recognized.

Not in a loud, obvious way— but in the quiet, human way:

Someone noticing. Someone saying, “this is good.” Someone making it clear that I belonged.

I didn’t have the language for it at the time.

So I translated it into effort.

Work harder. Stay later. Fix more things. Be useful enough that no one questions you.

It worked—on the surface.

But underneath, nothing was being resolved.

Luck, Misunderstood

If you looked at my life from the outside, it was simple:

A kid from Taiwan makes it to Silicon Valley.

Lucky.

That word came up a lot.

And I didn’t disagree.

But luck hides the more uncomfortable truth:

Getting in doesn’t mean you’re ready.

It just means you’re allowed in the room.

What you bring with you—your habits, your fears, your ways of making sense of the world— those don’t upgrade automatically.

They just get… exposed.

Learning What Was Never Taught

The hardest part wasn’t the work.

It was learning things that weren’t written anywhere:

  • How to read between the lines
  • How to ask for feedback without feeling like you're failing
  • How to separate your work from your worth
  • How to accept that “no negative feedback” might actually mean “you’re doing fine”

These weren’t technical problems.

They were human ones.

And I was underprepared.

The Quiet Realization

At some point, I started noticing a pattern.

The people who did well weren’t always the smartest.

They were the ones who seemed… at ease.

They could take feedback without shrinking. They could speak without over-calculating. They didn’t need constant confirmation to keep going.

It wasn’t confidence in the loud sense.

It was something steadier.

Something internal.

What This Chapter Really Was

I thought I came here to become a better engineer.

And I did.

But that wasn’t the real work.

The real work was more uncomfortable than that:

Learning that competence doesn’t automatically create security. Learning that achievement doesn’t automatically create belonging.

And slowly, painfully—

Learning that the thing I was chasing in work was never just about work.

I was more fortunate than I understood.

Not just because I made it here— but because this place revealed something I had been able to avoid seeing for a long time.

A need I didn’t know how to name.

A version of myself that needed more than success to feel whole.

The next chapter is where that realization starts to change how I live.
